Public Health / Community Health Worker

Public health is about protecting and improving the health of communities, not just treating one person at a time. People working in public health focus on prevention, awareness, and improving health at a population level.

What does a public health professional usually do?

  • Works to prevent diseases rather than treat them after they occur
  • Creates awareness about hygiene, nutrition, vaccination, and healthy habits
  • Studies health problems affecting communities
  • Helps plan and improve health programs
  • Works with governments, hospitals, and NGOs to improve public health
Real-life example
When many people in a village or area start getting fever and cold, a public health worker helps people learn simple habits like washing hands, keeping surroundings clean, and going to the health centre early to stay healthy.

You may enjoy this career if you:

  • Care about community health and social well-being
  • Like helping large groups of people, not just individuals
  • Are interested in prevention and awareness
  • Enjoy planning, organizing, and public interaction
  • Want to make a long-term impact on society

Courses in Public Health (India)

Bachelor of Public Health (BPH)

Duration: 3–4 years

Eligibility:

  • 12th standard (usually with Biology)
  • Minimum marks: around 45–50%

Master of Public Health (MPH)

Duration: 2 years

Eligibility:

  • Bachelor’s degree in Medical, Dental, Nursing, Allied Health, Life Sciences, or related fields

Entrance exams:

  • Merit-based or university/state-level entrance exams
  • Examples include TISSNET or institute-specific tests

Note: Admission process and entrance exams may vary by institute. Always check the official college or university website for exact details.

Where do public health professionals work?

  • Government health departments
  • Public health hospitals
  • NGOs and international organizations
  • Research institutions
  • Health programs and policy organizations

Important note

Public health professionals usually do not treat patients directly. Their work focuses on prevention, planning, education, and improving health systems.
