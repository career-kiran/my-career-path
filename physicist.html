Physicist

Who is a Physicist?

A physicist is someone who studies how light,energy,sound and motion work in nature.

They try to understand the basic rules of nature and use that knowledge to improve technology and solve real-world problems.

What does a Physicist usually do?

  • Studies natural forces like gravity, energy, heat, light, and electricity
  • Conducts experiments to understand how things move or behave
  • Uses maths and science to explain natural phenomena
  • Works on technologies like satellites, lasers, electronics, and medical machines
  • Develops new scientific ideas that can help society

Real Life Example (India)

A physicist working in ISRO helps rockets and satellites move correctly in space so they reach the right place.

Another physicist may work in a laboratory developing better medical imaging machines like MRI or X-ray systems.

You may enjoy this career if you:

  • Are curious about how things work
  • Enjoy solving logical or scientific problems
  • Like maths and science
  • Enjoy experiments and discovering new ideas

Courses to Become a Physicist

BSc Physics (3 years)

Eligibility:
10+2 with Physics, Chemistry, Mathematics
Minimum marks usually 50–60% (varies by college)

Entrance Exams:
CUET (for central universities)
State-level university entrance exams
Some colleges offer merit-based admission

MSc Physics (2 years)

Eligibility:
BSc Physics or related subject
Minimum marks usually 50–60%

Entrance Exams:
IIT JAM
CUET PG
University-level entrance exams

PhD in Physics (3–5 years)

Eligibility:
MSc Physics
Qualifying entrance exams

Entrance Exams:
CSIR-NET
GATE
JEST
University-level exams

Where do Physicists work?

  • ISRO (Indian Space Research Organisation)
  • DRDO (Defence Research and Development Organisation)
  • BARC (Bhabha Atomic Research Centre)
  • ICMR research labs
  • Universities and colleges
  • Private research laboratories
  • Technology and electronics companies

Important Note

Becoming a physicist usually requires higher studies (Master’s and PhD). If you enjoy understanding how the universe works and solving deep scientific questions, this career can be very meaningful and exciting.
